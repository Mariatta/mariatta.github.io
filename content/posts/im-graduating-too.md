I just ran the last hot lunch of the year for my youngest son's school.

He's graduating from middle school and will be going to high school in the next school year. Which makes me a graduating
parent too. And it means that it wasn't just the last hot lunch of the school year. It's the last one I'll ever run.

I've been running it for the last three years alongside a small group of other parent volunteers. Three school years of
handling orders, payments, receipts, buying and stockpiling pop drinks in bulk, vendor coordination, and a lot of things
that happen quietly in the background.

And now it is time to pass it on.

## How it started

In 2023, my son had just started at the middle school, moving up from elementary. Up until that moment, I had never
participated in any volunteering activities at his school. I have never attended any PAC meetings. I never really met or
socialized with the other parents. Whenever help was needed, I opted to just donate money instead of my time.

In December 2023, I decided to join one of the meetings, because I was feeling guilty about not being involved.

By then, the school still didn't have a hot lunch program. Another parent had run it before me, until their own kid
graduated and they moved on, the same way I'm moving on now. They never got to hand it off. No incoming parent had been
lined up, so when they left, the program left with them. The kids had gone the first four months of the year without
one. Not because nobody wanted it, but because nobody had stepped up to take over.

## How I got involved

Even before the school started, during the school summer break, there had been a call for volunteers to fill this role.
Other roles within the PAC were already taken. The hot lunch coordinator was the only one still open.

Yes, I saw the call. No, I didn't sign up. For two reasons.

**First**: I may have been new to volunteering at my son's school, but I've been volunteering elsewhere: as a conference
organizer (I was in the middle of organizing PyCon US), as a local meetup organizer, as an open source contributor and
maintainer. I was busy enough and so I didn't want to add yet another responsibility. I figured, that "surely someone
else will do it."

**Second**: I had no idea what it meant to run the hot lunch program. The whole call was barely a sentence — "we need
hot lunch coordinators" and nothing else. Is it just to show up and help distribute lunch? Or is it more involved than
that? Without any description of the work, no sense of the hours, no picture of what I'd be signing up for, how would
anybody respond to that? I don't like saying yes to something and then quietly back out a month in.

And I think that the reason the role was still open was because everyone else felt the same way. They're probably also
busy with their life, because they don't know what this role is about, and they all thought that "surely someone else
will do it". Everybody thinking the same thing, nobody did anything.

Other than the previous hot lunch coordinator themselves, nobody else really knew what the role was about, and maybe
that's why the role description was simply a "hot lunch coordinator". And since they had graduated, the knowledge left
with them.

During the PAC meeting, we were re-assured that, because the program has been running before, you could just load the
same menu, use the same vendor, just "pick it up where it left". They made it sound easy and simple and straightforward.

And so, I said yes, along with two other parents.

We met during the Christmas break, planned the lunch dates, contacted vendors, opened up the first lunch orders.

By January 2024, the hot lunch program was re-launched, and the first round of ordering closed with 200 orders of tacos
and burritos.

The first hot lunch distribution we did, was kinda chaotic. We made a lot of mistakes. We didn't have a system on how to
best distribute the meals and drinks. It took us almost 45 minutes to complete the distribution. Kids started their
lunch late.

We learned, and slowly, we improved.

## How it went

This year alone, we raised **$14,000** - **$15,000 in profit** just from the hot lunch program.

That money goes to the PAC. Which means it goes to the students, to help cover costs for field trips, activities,
classroom supplies. The hot lunch became one of the largest fundraising efforts for the PAC for the whole year.

It also helps parents because they get to have a break on hot lunch days. One less lunch to prepare in the morning.

Interestingly, the final hot lunch theme we did was the exact same hot lunch theme we did on our first run: a
taco-themed meal. Only this time, it only took us 15 minutes to complete the distribution. Talk about process
improvements over the years!

We started off running the hot lunch only once a month, and after we got the hang of it, we increased it to twice a
month, which raised more for the PAC.

## I'm passing it on, open source style

When I took over, we didn't know what we were supposed to be doing. We started from the beginning.

We re-learned the same things the previous volunteers must have already learned.

We made mistakes that had almost certainly been made before.

None of it had been captured anywhere, so all of that knowledge left when the previous parents left.

The next parent taking this over could repeat that exact cycle again: to start from nothing, spending an initial few
months re-learning, re-discovering everything the hard way, making mistakes.

Or I could make it easier.

So I wrote it all down. I created a Hot Lunch handbook, published openly with CC-BY-SA 4.0 license. Read it at:

**<https://mariatta.ca/lunch-handbook/>**

I've given the link to this handbook to the next parent stepping in, and I've shared it with the PAC, and gave explicit
permission to **please share it with anyone else who needs it**, future hot lunch volunteers, even if they're not in the
same school, because most schools in the area are also running their own version of the hot lunch program, using the
same online platform as ours, yet, there's no open knowledge about it.

I think this is one of those things that seemed simple: "it's just a hot lunch program".

But it is not simple.

Here are some stats about the handbook itself.

| What's in the handbook  |                     Count |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------: |
| Pages                   |                        14 |
| Words                   |                   ~12,000 |
| Email templates         |                         9 |
| Planning spreadsheet    | 1 spreadsheet with 7 tabs |
| Checklist steps         |                        37 |
| Hard-won "gotcha" notes |                        49 |

This is a full documentation of what a hot lunch coordinator does. It is not something that can be easily summarized
into one sentence, one paragraph. I don't know how such knowledge were passed before. Maybe it was not passed at all. Or
just verbally, relying on someone's memory. Everyone re-discovering and re-learning it all over again.

That is not sustainable.

And so, I wanted to leave a parting gift by creating this doc. The reader of this doc is probably just one-two people
from the school. But maybe, by having this doc available, future parents can learn from it. When the next parent also
graduated and need to pass along their knowledge, they could just pass along the handbook. When future parents wants to
know how the program works before committing to it, they could read it. No more confusion. No more guessing. No more
re-learning or starting from scratch.

## Thank you

On the last day of the hot lunch, I received this beautiful bouquet from the PAC team as their token of appreciation. I
was really surprised and touched by this gesture. I didn't expect it at all. It was a really nice way to end the hot
lunch program for the year, and to mark the end of my time as a hot lunch coordinator. It made me feel appreciated and
valued for the work I did, even though it was mostly invisible labor behind the scenes. It also made me feel grateful
for being part of such a supportive community of parents and volunteers at the school.
